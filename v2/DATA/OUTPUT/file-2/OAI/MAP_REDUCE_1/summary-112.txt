1. A residential mortgage exposure is considered when the exposure is 90 days or more past due, the banking organization has taken a partial charge-off or write-down of principal, or a distressed restructuring of the exposure has been agreed upon. This includes forgiveness or postponement of principal, interest, or fees, term extension, or an interest rate reduction.
2. For a real estate exposure that is not a residential mortgage exposure, the obligor's credit obligation to the banking organization is considered if it is 90 days or more past due, or if the banking organization determines that the obligor is unlikely to pay its credit obligations in full.
3. An exposure that is not a residential mortgage exposure may be considered no longer unlikely to pay its credit obligations in full if the banking organization determines the obligor is speculative grade or investment grade.
4. Grade A and B bank exposures are defined by the depository institution, foreign bank, or credit union's investment grade and capital ratios, with certain conditions that may disqualify them. Grade C bank exposure is a bank exposure that does not qualify as a Grade A or B bank exposure.
